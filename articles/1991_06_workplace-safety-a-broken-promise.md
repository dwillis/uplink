The federal government said King's employer neglected to brace the unfinished Midas Muffler Shop wall on that July 1988 day near Atlanta. It imposed a fine for two "serious" violations of workplace safety standards.

The fine was $2.

Twenty years ago the federal government pledged to assure safe and healthful working conditions for all Americans.

But an eight-month Dayton Daily News examination found the system created to do this fails. It fails because the government doesn't compel employers to make safety a priority. It fails because employers have little fear of serious punishment when workers are killed or maimed.

The Daily News reviewed thousands of computer records from the Occupational Safety and Health Administration, the federal agency overseeing workplace safety and health. Findings:

- Employers routinely pay small fines when cited for safety lapses, even when workers are killed. The median OSHA fine for such accidents from 1972 through 1990 was $500, though penalties have increased.
